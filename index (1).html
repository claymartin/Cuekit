Physically ready. Mentally scattered.

Reflection doesn’t stick

Athletes reflect after matches, but those notes rarely turn into a clear plan for the next one.

Routines feel forced

Coach-assigned routines can feel generic or controlling, so athletes drop them under pressure.

Too many ideas, no simple cues

They know about breathing, self-talk, imagery, and more, but they don’t know which to use tonight—or how to remember it on court.

Three pieces, one loop.

Step 1 – Reflect in your journal

After practices or matches, athletes spend 3–5 minutes rating confidence, anxiety, focus, and physical tension from 1–5 and noting key moments.

Step 2 – Build your cue plan

On match day, they enter those scores online, answer a few quick questions, and get one primary mental technique plus a simple routine.

Step 3 – Wear your cues on court

Color-coded shoe straps turn that plan into visible cues, so the athlete can recall it between points with a quick glance.

Your cue colors.

Blue – Breathing: Calm your body and reset after mistakes.
Yellow – Imagery: Picture yourself executing plays before they happen.
Orange – Self-Talk: Use short phrases to boost confidence and cut through negative thoughts.
Purple – Focus Cues: Lock attention onto the one thing that matters this rally.
Brown – Muscle Relaxation: Release tension so your body moves freely.
White – Reset Routine: Flush the last point and start the next one clean.

Built around real players like Alexis.

Collegiate libero, struggles with overthinking after early passing errors, feels tension in legs during serve receive.

Alexis uses the journal to note her ratings and key moments. On match day, she builds her cue plan online, getting orange and brown straps to wear. Between points, she glances at orange for self-talk and brown for relaxation.
